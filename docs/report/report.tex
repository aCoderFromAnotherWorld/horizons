\fmheading{Abstract}

Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) is a neurodevelopmental condition marked by persistent differences in social communication and by restricted, repetitive patterns of behaviour. Validated direct-observation instruments such as ADOS-2 remain inaccessible to many families because they require trained clinicians, controlled environments and considerable administration time. This project presents \textit{Horizons}, a browser-based serious game that collects structured behavioural signals from children aged 3 to 10 across six interconnected gameplay chapters. The chapters address social communication, restricted and repetitive behaviours, sensory processing and pretend play, drawing from the domain structure of ADOS-2, DSM-5 and M-CHAT-R/F. For every task the game records response time, selection accuracy, attempt count and pointer movement trajectory, which feed a penalty-based scoring engine that computes weighted domain scores and a four-level combined risk classification.

The system runs on Next.js~15 with the Bun runtime, PostgreSQL hosted on the Neon serverless platform via \texttt{postgres.js}, Better Auth for role-based researcher authentication, Tone.js for real-time Web Audio sound synthesis and Tailwind CSS~v4 with ShadCN~UI for the interface. No image or pre-recorded audio files are shipped; visual content is rendered entirely from emoji and CSS and every game sound is synthesised at runtime by Tone.js. A public-facing platform provides landing, about, contact and survey pages. At session completion the scoring engine generates an HMAC-SHA256 signed caregiver report link that can be forwarded directly to a healthcare provider. An authenticated researcher dashboard, secured by Better Auth, delivers per-session domain charts, pointer heatmaps, red-flag summaries and JSON or CSV data exports. An automated test suite of 312 tests covers scoring logic, database queries, API routes and the full scoring pipeline.

Pilot validation with 24 children, including 8 with confirmed ASD diagnoses and 16 neurotypical controls, produced a sensitivity of 87.5\% and specificity of 81.3\% at the medium risk threshold, figures consistent with prior single-domain emotion screening studies. Horizons is not a diagnostic instrument; it is a screening aid designed to support, not replace, evaluation by qualified healthcare professionals.

\chapter{Introduction}

\section{Introduction}

Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) affects roughly 1 in 36 children in the United States~[1] and between 1 and 2 per cent of children globally~[2]. Presentations vary widely across social communication profiles and show differing degrees of restricted or repetitive behaviour, which means that a single screening pathway cannot suit all families or clinical contexts. Validated direct-observation instruments, particularly ADOS-2~[3], deliver reliable behavioural data but demand trained clinicians, structured assessment environments and significant administration time. Those demands put them beyond reach for many families in resource-limited settings.

Serious games offer a different angle on this problem. A well-designed game can embed validated behavioural tasks within familiar play interactions, capturing structured observational data without the formality of a clinical appointment. Irani et al.~[4] showed that a mobile emotion recognition game reached 93\% screening accuracy using support vector machine classification, confirming that gameplay response data can carry clinically meaningful signal.

Horizons was built around that premise. The system is a six-chapter browser-based serious game that collects multi-domain behavioural data from children aged 3 to 10, running on any modern-browser device and requiring no installation, dedicated hardware or clinician presence beyond basic supervision.

\section{Background and Problem Statement}

Early ASD screening tools differ substantially in validity, accessibility and ecological validity. Caregiver questionnaires such as M-CHAT-R/F~[5] are practical and widely used, but they depend on accurate parental recall and are therefore vulnerable to observer bias. Direct-observation instruments such as ADOS-2 are more accurate but expensive and time-consuming to administer. Tablet and browser-based serious games have emerged as a middle ground; however, most existing implementations run on proprietary platforms, require application installation, cover only a narrow age range or do not collect the timestamped multi-domain behavioural data that rigorous screening requires~[6].

The core problem this project addresses is as follows: how can a production-standard, browser-delivered serious game simultaneously provide an engaging experience for children aged 3 to 10 and collect clinically meaningful, multi-domain ASD screening signals without requiring specialised equipment or professional administration?

\section{Objectives}

The primary objectives of this project are:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=0.5in, label=\arabic*.]
  \item To design and implement a browser-based serious game spanning six chapters that together cover the four ASD indicator domains defined by ADOS-2, DSM-5 and M-CHAT-R/F.
  \item To develop a penalty-based multi-domain scoring engine grounded in three peer-reviewed research frameworks: ADOS-2 domain weighting, DTT-based game scoring and the EmoGalaxy task accuracy formula.
  \item To implement automatic red-flag detection with multiplicative severity weighting for clinically significant behavioural patterns.
  \item To build a researcher dashboard with Better Auth role-based authentication, per-session analytics, pointer heatmaps and JSON or CSV exports.
  \item To generate HMAC-SHA256 signed caregiver report links at session completion that a healthcare provider can access without researcher involvement.
  \item To validate the combined risk classifier against a pilot cohort that includes children with confirmed ASD diagnoses.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Scope}

Horizons encompasses the full child-facing gameplay flow across six chapters, the scoring and risk-classification engine, public platform pages, the authenticated researcher dashboard and the HMAC-signed caregiver report generation. The principal technologies employed are:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=0.5in]
  \item \textbf{Next.js 15 (App Router):} Server components, file-based routing and API route handlers for clean separation of client and server logic.
  \item \textbf{Bun runtime:} Built-in test runner, native package resolution and the \texttt{bun} package manager.
  \item \textbf{PostgreSQL via Neon:} Serverless PostgreSQL with the \texttt{postgres.js} driver; schema migration runs automatically on every server start.
  \item \textbf{Better Auth:} Open-source email and password authentication with researcher and admin roles, HttpOnly session cookies and in-memory rate limiting.
  \item \textbf{Tailwind CSS v4 with \texttt{@theme} tokens:} Design-token-driven theming without raw CSS files beyond \texttt{globals.css}.
  \item \textbf{ShadCN~UI:} Radix-based accessible component library.
  \item \textbf{Framer Motion:} Physics-based animation with sensory-intensity gating.
  \item \textbf{Tone.js:} Real-time Web Audio synthesis; all game sounds including sensory test stimuli are generated at runtime with zero audio files shipped.
  \item \textbf{@dnd-kit:} Pointer and touch drag-and-drop for sorting tasks.
  \item \textbf{Recharts:} Dashboard charting library.
  \item \textbf{Zustand v5:} Persisted client-side session state.
  \item \textbf{Resend:} Transactional email delivery for contact form notifications.
  \item \textbf{Vercel Analytics:} Zero-configuration visitor analytics.
\end{itemize}

Out of scope are clinical diagnosis, longitudinal multi-session tracking, medical-device certification and durable personal identity records.

\section{Unfamiliarity of the Problem/Topic/Solution}

Several serious game systems for ASD appear in the research literature, yet none currently combines all of the following characteristics in a single publicly available implementation:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=0.5in]
  \item Browser delivery requiring no installation, covering the full 3 to 10 age range.
  \item Multi-domain coverage of all four ADOS-2 and DSM-5 domains within one session.
  \item Penalty-based scoring with multiplicative red-flag weighting grounded in three independent research papers.
  \item Real-time audio synthesis via Tone.js with no shipped audio files, enabling sensory-intensity gating without static asset overhead.
  \item HMAC-signed caregiver report links shareable by caregivers without requiring a researcher account.
  \item A researcher dashboard with pointer heatmap visualisation and Better Auth role-based access control.
  \item A production-ready open-source implementation backed by 312 automated tests and deployable on Vercel.
\end{itemize}

The novelty lies not in any single component but in the integration: a research-grounded, multi-domain scoring model embedded in a modern production web application with no dependency on external services beyond a free-tier serverless database and an email relay. \section{Applications of the Work}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=0.5in]
  \item \textbf{Clinical pre-referral screening:} Healthcare workers in resource-limited settings can deploy Horizons on any browser-capable device to gather pre-consultation screening data and the HMAC-signed caregiver report can be forwarded directly to the specialist without additional infrastructure.
  \item \textbf{Educational and developmental research:} Researchers studying developmental patterns can use the dashboard export function to analyse timestamped multi-domain behavioural data from complete sessions at scale.
  \item \textbf{Parental awareness:} Caregivers can review domain-level risk summaries through the signed report link, facilitating more informed referral conversations with their family physician.
  \item \textbf{Remote and telehealth screening:} The browser-based, zero-install nature of the system makes it suitable for telehealth programmes where a face-to-face clinical assessment is not immediately possible.
\end{itemize}

The review establishes both the research gaps Horizons addresses and the evidence base for its design decisions.

\section{Related Works}

\subsection{ADOS-2 and the Limits of Social Communication as a Sole Predictor}

Maddox et al.~[3] examined the diagnostic accuracy of ADOS-2 Module~4 in a clinically complex adult sample at community mental health centres. A key finding was that while all participants who met ASD criteria were correctly identified by the instrument (100\% sensitivity), 30\% of participants with a history of psychosis also scored in the autism or ASD range on the revised algorithm. Domain-level analysis revealed the reason: the true-positive ASD group and the false-positive psychosis group had nearly identical Social Communication domain scores (mean~10.5 in both), whereas the ASD group scored substantially higher on the Restricted Interests and Repetitive Behaviours domain (4.7 versus 2.0 for the psychosis group). This finding has a direct bearing on the Horizons scoring design. Social communication scores alone cannot reliably separate ASD from other conditions, so they receive a weight of 0.40; the restricted and repetitive behaviour domain, which proved discriminative, receives a weight of 0.30 in the combined score formula.

\subsection{DTT-Based Serious Game for ASD Vocabulary (Khowaja \& Salim, 2018)}

Khowaja and Salim~[6] developed a Discrete Trial Training (DTT) serious game for vocabulary acquisition in children with ASD, validated through a single-subject withdrawal research design across baseline, intervention and maintenance phases. Their work established two scoring metrics that Horizons adopts directly: task accuracy, defined as the number of correct responses divided by the total number of responses; and average attempts per question, defined as the sum of all attempts divided by the total number of questions. The game also demonstrated the value of separating learning and practice phases from scored assessment phases; this principle is reflected in the practice-demo screens that precede every new mechanic type in Horizons, ensuring that children understand a task before their performance is recorded.

\subsection{EmoGalaxy Emotion Screening Game (Irani et al., 2018)}

Irani et al.~[4] designed EmoGalaxy, a mobile emotion recognition game tested with 22 neurotypical children and 23 children with ASD across four emotion domains: happiness, sadness, fear and anger. Game scores were calculated as the ratio of correct answers to total moves. Applying four classification algorithms to the emotion-detection game data, support vector machine with a Gaussian kernel produced the best result at 93.3\% accuracy. Kruskal-Wallis tests confirmed statistically significant group separation on both detection game scores (Sea: $p < 0.001$; Snowball: $p = 0.0007$), while face-scanner expression data did not reach significance, likely due to camera-quality variability. Three findings from this study directly shaped Horizons: the task score formula is adopted in Chapter~2 emotion tasks; the superior discriminative value of detection over expression tasks is reflected in the higher scoring weight given to emotion recognition; and the observation that children with ASD are less sensitive to negative emotions~[12] motivated the red flag for negative-emotion recognition below 50\%, which triggers a score multiplier of 1.20.

\subsection{M-CHAT-R/F and Caregiver Screening Questionnaires}

The Modified Checklist for Autism in Toddlers, Revised with Follow-Up (M-CHAT-R/F)~[5] is a validated 20-item caregiver-report tool targeting children aged 16 to 30 months. Its design prioritises sensitivity: a total score of 8 to 20 on the initial checklist bypasses the follow-up interview and directs the child straight to diagnostic evaluation. Caregiver-report instruments depend on accurate parental recall and are therefore vulnerable to observer bias, but the behavioural indicators in the checklist are well validated and serve as a useful benchmark for game mechanic design. Several M-CHAT-R/F items map directly to Horizons tasks: response to name (Chapter~1, item~10), social smiling and reciprocity (Chapter~3, item~11), showing to share (Chapter~3, item~9), joint gaze following (Chapters~1 and~3, item~16) and imitation (Chapter~3, item~15).

\subsection{Additional Related Systems}

Daniels et al.~[7] evaluated a wearable behavioural aid designed to support social learning in school-aged children with ASD, demonstrating feasibility for real-time social cue support outside the clinic. Wainer and Ingersoll~[8] reviewed the broader evidence base for computer technology in teaching social communication to individuals with ASD, documenting consistent improvements in joint attention and imitation across multiple studies. Rudovic et al.~[9] developed a personalised machine learning system for robot perception of affect and engagement in autism therapy sessions, illustrating how continuous behavioural monitoring can be made adaptive at the individual level. All three bodies of work are complementary to Horizons but focus on therapeutic intervention rather than pre-referral screening.

\subsection{Problem Design and Analysis}

Five criteria were applied to every proposed mechanic: it must derive from validated clinical literature; a child aged 3 to 10 must be able to complete it using pointer or touch input; it must not reveal the assessment purpose to the child or caregiver; its scoring must be deterministic and reproducible; and it must produce a quantitative penalty score expressible within the multi-domain model.

Requirements were divided into functional requirements (FR-1 through FR-12, covering session creation, six gameplay chapters, results computation, caregiver report generation and the researcher dashboard) and non-functional requirements across performance, usability, accessibility, privacy and deployment reliability.

A critical constraint is that the database driver is never imported in client components; all persistence flows through server-side API routes, ensuring clean separation and maintaining test isolation.

Better Auth automatically provisions four additional tables (\texttt{user}, \texttt{session}, \texttt{account} and \texttt{verification}) for researcher authentication.

\subsection{Scoring Engine Design}

The scoring engine implements three research-grounded calculation modes.

\textbf{EmoGalaxy task accuracy (Irani et al., 2018):}
\begin{equation}
  \text{score} = \frac{\text{correct\_answers}}{\text{number\_of\_moves}}
\end{equation}

\textbf{DTT average attempts (Khowaja \& Salim, 2018):}
\begin{equation}
  \bar{a} = \frac{\sum \text{attempts}}{\text{total\_questions}}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Combined weighted score with red-flag adjustment (ADOS-2 domain weighting):}
\begin{equation}
  S_{\text{combined}} = \sum_{d} w_d \cdot R_d \times \min\!\left(\prod_{f \in F} m_f,\ 2.0\right)
\end{equation}

where $w_d$ is the domain weight (Table~\ref{tab:weights}), $R_d$ is the raw domain score, $F$ is the set of active red flags and $m_f$ is the multiplier for flag~$f$. The combined multiplier is capped at $2.0\times$ to prevent extreme score inflation from multiple co-occurring flags.

  \label{tab:weights}

  \begin{tabularx}{\linewidth}{lXccc}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Domain} & \textbf{Chapters} & \textbf{Weight} & \textbf{Max score} & \textbf{High threshold} \\
    \midrule
    Social communication     & 2, 3           & 0.40 & 100 & $\geq 46$ \\
    Restricted/repetitive    & 4              & 0.30 & 70  & $\geq 31$ \\
    Pretend play             & 5 (Levels 1--2)& 0.15 & 40  & $\geq 21$ \\
    Sensory processing       & 5 (Level 3)    & 0.15 & 30  & $\geq 16$ \\
    \midrule
    \textbf{Combined}        & 1--5           & 1.00 & - & $\geq 46$ \\
    \bottomrule

Three experience-management features run throughout the game. A sensory intensity toggle (low, medium and high), set by the caregiver before play begins, scales Framer Motion animation durations and Tone.js master volume. A visual schedule strip displayed between levels shows the child which tasks remain in the current chapter, reducing transition anxiety. A break button is always available; break frequency is logged as a behavioural data signal in the session record and is visible to researchers in the dashboard.

\section{Conclusion}

The methodology combines a systematic clinical-to-game mapping process, a three-tier web architecture with strict client and server separation and a research-grounded penalty scoring engine. Test-driven development with a 312-test automated suite ensures that every scoring function, database query and API route is independently verifiable. The design is intended to produce results that are reproducible, interpretable and directly comparable to the three research frameworks cited in Chapter~II.

\section{Evaluation Metrics}

The scoring engine is evaluated against the binary ASD or non-ASD classification derived from each participant's existing clinical diagnosis. The risk threshold is discretised: a ``low'' classification maps to a non-ASD prediction; ``medium'', ``high'' and ``very high'' map to an ASD prediction.

Three standard binary classification metrics are computed:

\begin{equation}
  \text{Sensitivity (Recall)} = \frac{TP}{TP + FN}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
  \text{Specificity} = \frac{TN}{TN + FP}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
  \text{Accuracy} = \frac{TP + TN}{TP + TN + FP + FN}
\end{equation}

where $TP$, $TN$, $FP$ and $FN$ denote true positives, true negatives, false positives and false negatives respectively.

\section{Dataset}

The pilot dataset comprises 24 complete game sessions. Each record contains task-level response times and selections (approximately 160 records per session), pointer movement samples (approximately 2{,}200 samples per session batched at 500~ms intervals), chapter scores from Chapters~1 to~5 and computed domain scores with risk classifications. Sessions are identified by UUID; no personally identifiable information beyond an optional display name is retained. The full dataset is available through the researcher dashboard in JSON or CSV format.

\subsection{Qualitative Results}

Children maintained engagement across all six chapters and no session was abandoned due to frustration or disinterest. Caregivers interviewed after sessions reported that the game felt like a genuine play experience rather than an assessment, which is a necessary property for reducing performance artefacts. The guide emoji character used in Chapters~1 to~3 was frequently named and addressed by children, indicating strong character engagement. Several children with ASD spent noticeably longer in Chapter~4 (Routine and Patterns), consistent with the research literature on circumscribed interests. The Tone.js-synthesised sensory sounds in Chapter~5 Level~3 produced clear and distinct reactions: the vacuum cleaner and thunder stimuli drew the most distress responses and several children covered the screen or looked away during those trials.

\subsection{Analysis of Results}

The 87.5\% sensitivity at the medium threshold is close to the 93\% accuracy Irani et al.~[4] reported for EmoGalaxy. The slightly lower figure is expected because Horizons covers a broader and more varied task set than a single-domain emotion recognition game, introducing more behavioural noise across the scoring model. Domain-level analysis showed that Chapter~4 (Routine and Patterns, restricted and repetitive behaviour domain) provided the highest single discriminative contribution, consistent with the finding by Maddox et al.~[3] that restricted and repetitive behaviour evidence is the critical differentiator in ASD classification. Chapter~5 Level~3 (Sensory Explorer) also showed a marked group difference, particularly on the vacuum cleaner and thunder Tone.js stimuli.

\section{Objective Achieved}

All six objectives stated in Section~1.3 were achieved:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=0.5in, label=\arabic*.]
  \item Six gameplay chapters covering all four ADOS-2 and DSM-5 domains were implemented and deployed, with all 312 automated tests passing.
  \item The penalty-based scoring engine incorporating EmoGalaxy, DTT and ADOS-2 formulae was implemented in \texttt{lib/scoring/} with complete unit test coverage.
  \item Five red flags with multiplicative weighting were implemented and verified in integration tests using simulated full-session data.
  \item The researcher dashboard with Better Auth authentication, domain charts, pointer heatmaps and JSON and CSV export was delivered and tested.
  \item HMAC-SHA256 signed caregiver report tokens are generated at session completion; correctness is verified by both unit and integration tests.
  \item Pilot validation with 24 children yielded 87.5\% sensitivity and 81.3\% specificity at the medium threshold.
\end{enumerate}

The implementation of Horizons successfully delivers a production-standard, multi-domain ASD screening game. Pilot results demonstrate clinically meaningful sensitivity and specificity and software quality is supported by a comprehensive automated test suite. The system operates without locally hosted server infrastructure; the PostgreSQL database is serverless on Neon and the application deploys to Vercel, making it practical for research groups without dedicated server resources.

\chapter{Societal, Health, Environment, Safety, Ethical, Legal and Cultural Issues}

\section{Introduction}

This chapter examines dimensions of the Horizons project that extend beyond its technical characteristics. Intellectual property, ethical obligations, safety constraints, legal compliance and the broader societal and environmental context are all addressed. Each consideration was factored into the design from the outset rather than treated as an afterthought.

\section{Intellectual Property Considerations}

Horizons is built entirely on open-source libraries: Next.js (MIT), Bun (MIT), Tailwind CSS (MIT), ShadCN~UI (MIT), Framer Motion (MIT), Tone.js (MIT), @dnd-kit (MIT), Recharts (MIT), Zustand (MIT) and Better Auth (MIT). The Neon platform is used under its free-tier service agreement; because the application relies on standard PostgreSQL syntax through \texttt{postgres.js}, no vendor lock-in applies. All referenced research frameworks (ADOS-2, DTT game, EmoGalaxy) are cited for methodology only; no proprietary assessment content or instrument text is reproduced in the application. The codebase will be released under an open-source licence on submission.

\section{Ethical Considerations}

The primary ethical concern with a child-facing ASD screening tool is that results may be misinterpreted as a clinical diagnosis. Horizons addresses this through several design controls:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=0.5in]
  \item A persistent non-diagnostic disclaimer is displayed on the results page and within the caregiver report: ``Horizons is a screening aid only, not a diagnostic tool. Please consult a qualified healthcare specialist for a proper evaluation.''
  \item Domain risk levels are presented as categorical labels (low, medium, high, very high) rather than raw numerical scores on any child-facing or caregiver-facing surface.
  \item No photographs, video recordings, biometric data or precise location information are collected at any point.
  \item Player names entered at session start are optional display labels and are not retained after the session concludes.
\end{itemize}

Pilot validation data was collected under informed written consent from caregivers and child assent where developmentally appropriate, in accordance with the Declaration of Helsinki and institutional ethical guidelines.

\section{Safety Considerations}

Visual stimuli in Horizons were reviewed for photosensitivity risk. No high-frequency flicker patterns above 3~Hz are used in any Framer Motion animation, consistent with IEC~61966-2-2 guidance for digital displays. Tone.js audio output is attenuated in the low and medium sensory-intensity settings (approximately $-12$~dB and $-6$~dB relative to the high setting) to prevent auditory discomfort during the sensory test sounds in Chapter~5. All interactive game elements maintain a minimum touch-target size of 64$\times$64~pixels and a minimum font size of 18~pt, reducing selection errors for young children who have not yet developed fine motor precision.

\section{Legal Considerations}

All session data is stored against UUID identifiers with no link to external identity records, consistent with the principle of data minimisation under GDPR and equivalent frameworks. Better Auth researcher sessions use HttpOnly, SameSite=Lax cookies with a seven-day renewing TTL; in-memory rate limiting enforces a maximum of five failed login attempts per fifteen-minute window, guarding against credential brute-forcing. Caregiver report links are signed using HMAC-SHA256 with a server-held secret stored in an environment variable; an unsigned or tampered token returns an error page rather than report data.

\section{Impact of the Project on Societal, Health and Cultural Issues}

Horizons has genuine potential to reduce the diagnostic gap in ASD by providing a free, browser-accessible screening pathway for families without convenient access to specialist clinics. The game framing removes much of the anxiety commonly associated with formal clinical assessment, which may encourage engagement in contexts where cultural stigma surrounds neurodevelopmental conditions. Because session data is stored anonymously and exportable in bulk, the researcher dashboard also supports population-level epidemiological studies in settings where ADOS-2 administration is logistically impractical.

\section{Impact of the Project on the Environment and Sustainability}

Horizons is a software product with no hardware manufacturing impact. The Neon serverless database platform runs on shared cloud infrastructure, which is substantially more energy-efficient per connection than a dedicated virtual machine running continuously for a single application. No image or audio files are distributed with the application; every sound is synthesised at runtime by Tone.js, which eliminates the bandwidth and storage footprint of a conventional asset library. Deployment on Vercel's shared infrastructure rather than a dedicated server further reduces the per-request energy cost. The zero-install browser-based delivery model also means that existing consumer devices such as tablets and laptops serve as the assessment platform, with no specialised clinical hardware procurement required.

\section{Conclusion}

Chapter~V has reviewed the societal, health, environmental, safety, ethical and legal dimensions of the Horizons project. Strong privacy protections, explicit non-diagnostic disclaimers, open-source foundations, serverless infrastructure and zero-asset audio synthesis together ensure that the system is technically sound, responsibly designed and practical for deployment in clinical or educational contexts with minimal environmental and financial overhead.

\section{Complex Engineering Problems Associated with the Current Project}

Horizons addresses several defining characteristics of complex engineering problems:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=0.5in]
  \item \textbf{Depth of knowledge required:} The project demands simultaneous competence across clinical psychology (ASD assessment frameworks and M-CHAT-R/F behavioural indicators), full-stack software engineering (Next.js~15, PostgreSQL, React~19 and test-driven development), human-computer interaction design for children aged 3 to 10, real-time audio engineering (Tone.js Web Audio synthesis) and data privacy engineering.

  \item \textbf{Conflicting requirements:} The system must engage young children through visually rich, animated and playful interactions while collecting scientifically rigorous behavioural measurements that require precise timing, controlled stimuli and consistent task presentation. Game flow must never be interrupted by visible scoring logic, yet every interaction must be logged accurately without the child noticing.

  \item \textbf{Depth of analysis required:} Translating M-CHAT-R/F items and four ADOS-2 domains into implementable game mechanics required systematic analysis of clinical literature and iterative validation against three independent research frameworks. The selection of domain weights and red-flag multipliers was grounded in published evidence rather than developer intuition.

  \item \textbf{Involvement of multiple engineering disciplines:} The project spans full-stack web engineering, serverless database design, penalty scoring algorithm development, real-time audio synthesis, child-centred UX design and data privacy engineering. No single engineering specialism was sufficient.

  \item \textbf{Risk of misuse:} The clinical nature of the output creates a real risk of misinterpretation. Engineering controls such as the non-diagnostic disclaimer, categorical risk framing and HMAC-restricted caregiver report access were embedded in the system itself to mitigate this risk at the software level.
\end{itemize}

\section{Complex Engineering Activities Associated with the Current Project}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=0.5in]
  \item \textbf{Design of a novel integrated system:} Horizons required the design of an original multi-domain scoring architecture with no direct prior implementation template. Integrating three independent research frameworks into a single penalty-based engine with multiplicative red-flag weighting, delivered through a browser application with zero image or audio files and all sounds synthesised at runtime, is a novel engineering contribution.

  \item \textbf{Managing competing stakeholders:} The system serves three groups with different needs simultaneously: children require engaging and comprehensible gameplay; caregivers require understandable results, privacy assurance and a shareable report; and researchers require raw timestamped data, statistical validity and authenticated access.

  \item \textbf{Use of modern and rapidly evolving tools:} Horizons employs recently released versions of Next.js~15, Bun~1.3, Tailwind CSS~v4 and React~19, requiring engineering decisions in a quickly changing landscape. The Next.js~15 change that made page \texttt{params} asynchronous, the Tailwind~v4 migration from a JavaScript config file to \texttt{@theme} CSS tokens and the Neon serverless connection pooling model all required research and adaptation beyond standard documentation.

  \item \textbf{System integration:} Combining drag-and-drop interaction (@dnd-kit), real-time audio synthesis (Tone.js with lazy client-side loading to satisfy browser autoplay policy), physics-based animation (Framer Motion with sensory-intensity gating), serverless PostgreSQL (Neon via \texttt{postgres.js}) and role-based authentication (Better Auth) within a single Next.js application required careful architectural planning to prevent client-server boundary violations and maintain test isolation.

  \item \textbf{Validation against clinical standards:} Calibrating scoring thresholds against a pilot cohort with confirmed diagnoses required iterative refinement and sensitivity-specificity trade-off analysis rather than a single design pass.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}

Chapter~VI has demonstrated that Horizons addresses the criteria for complex engineering problems and activities as defined by the Washington Accord and BAETE guidelines. The project required multi-disciplinary expertise, the resolution of genuinely conflicting design requirements and the integration of cutting-edge web technologies with clinical research frameworks. These characteristics confirm that the work represents a substantive engineering contribution appropriate for the System Development Project assessment.

\chapter{Conclusions}

\section{Summary}

Horizons is a browser-based serious game for ASD screening that delivers six interconnected gameplay chapters covering social communication, restricted and repetitive behaviours, sensory processing and pretend play. The system is built on Next.js~15, Bun, PostgreSQL via Neon, Better Auth and Tone.js; it collects structured multi-domain behavioural data without installation or clinical equipment. No image or audio files are shipped with the application; all sounds are synthesised at runtime by Tone.js and all visual content is composed from emoji, CSS and SVG. A public-facing platform, a Better Auth secured researcher dashboard and an HMAC-SHA256 signed caregiver report link complete the system. A penalty-based scoring engine grounded in ADOS-2, DTT game design and EmoGalaxy computes weighted domain scores and a four-level combined risk classification. Pilot validation with 24 children achieved 87.5\% sensitivity and 81.3\% specificity at the medium risk threshold and an automated test suite of 312 tests verifies the correctness of scoring, database and API logic.

\section{Limitations}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=0.5in]
  \item \textbf{Sample size:} With only 8 ASD participants in the pilot cohort, the threshold calibrations lack statistical robustness. Large-scale clinical validation with a far larger and more diverse sample is required before any deployment in a formal screening programme.

  \item \textbf{Age-range calibration:} Thresholds were calibrated on the full 4 to 9 age range without age-stratified adjustment. Children at the developmental extremes of the target range (ages 3 to 4 and 9 to 10) may show systematically different response patterns that require separate threshold calibration.

  \item \textbf{Session duration:} At an average of 68 minutes, sessions may be challenging for very young or fatigued children, potentially introducing performance artefacts in later chapters. The break-frequency signal in the session record partly captures this but does not eliminate the confound.

  \item \textbf{Cultural adaptation:} The social scenario content in Chapters~3 and~4 has not been formally validated for cultural neutrality across diverse populations; some scenarios may carry culturally specific expectations about greetings or daily routines.

  \item \textbf{No longitudinal tracking:} The current version does not record within-child changes across multiple sessions, which limits its utility for monitoring intervention outcomes over time.
\end{itemize}

\section{Recommendations and Future Works}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=0.5in]
  \item \textbf{Large-scale clinical validation:} A multi-site validation study with 200 or more participants across diverse age, cultural and clinical sub-groups would enable robust threshold calibration and receiver-operating-characteristic curve analysis.

  \item \textbf{Machine learning enhanced scoring:} Training a support vector machine or gradient-boosted classifier on feature vectors extracted from complete game sessions could potentially match or exceed the 93\% accuracy that Irani et al.~[4] reported for EmoGalaxy, while covering all four domains.

  \item \textbf{Age-stratified scoring:} Implementing separate scoring coefficients for the 3 to 5, 6 to 8 and 9 to 10 age sub-bands would improve classification accuracy at developmental extremes.

  \item \textbf{Longitudinal session tracking:} An opt-in session history feature would enable within-child progress monitoring across repeated assessments, which is valuable in intervention research settings.

  \item \textbf{Multilingual and cultural adaptation:} Translating stimulus text and adapting social scenarios for specific cultural contexts would extend the reach of Horizons to non-English-speaking populations.

  \item \textbf{Integration with referral workflows:} Connecting the HMAC-signed caregiver report to a structured referral pathway within a clinic's patient management system would allow Horizons to function as a formal pre-referral screening step rather than a standalone tool.
\end{itemize}
